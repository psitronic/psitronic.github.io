Making AI work outside the lab

Here's what I've noticed after fifteen years in this field: most AI projects don't fail because the model is bad. They fail because nobody thought through what happens when you put it in front of real users, real data, and real constraints. That's where I come in.

I spent seven years at MOSTLY AI (Vienna, Austria), starting as a developer and eventually leading the AI research team. The main thing I built there was a generative model for synthetic data — the kind banks and healthcare companies use when they need realistic data but can't touch the real thing for privacy reasons. It had to be fast, it had to be accurate, and it had to come with mathematical privacy guarantees. No shortcuts.

Along the way, I worked on systems that combine different types of data — tables, text, images, time series — into something coherent. I built tools that let you inject business rules into neural networks (because sometimes the model needs to know that Tuesdays are different from Wednesdays) and benchmark deep learning models. And I spent a lot of time on the optimization stuff: getting models to train across multiple GPUs, making inference fast enough to be useful, building the testing pipelines that catch problems before users do.

Before all this, I was a physicist — designing control systems for experiments that ran at temperatures close to absolute zero. Different field, but the same lesson: when your software has to work, you learn to build it differently. PhD from Moscow State University. Currently based in Vienna.
